Attack Rolls — Base Rules
Pokémon Rule
Pokémon do not add any bonuses to their attack rolls or damage rolls unless explicitly stated by a Move or Trait card. All attacks use the standard 2d12 roll with Hope or Fear as normal.

When a Pokémon uses a standard attack, it deals damage of its own type. If a Pokémon has two types, the player or trainer chooses which type the attack deals at the time of the attack.

A Pokémon's standard attack starts at Melee range unless otherwise specified by a Move or Trait card.
Core Daggerheart Adaptations
Hope & Fear — the dual dice system
Core
Players roll 2d12 — one Hope die, one Fear die. The higher result determines success; which die is higher determines narrative tone. Hope = player advantage, Fear = GM gets a Fear token to spend. Adaptation: Fear tokens can be spent to trigger wild Pokémon ambushes, weather events, or enemy trainer interruptions mid-scene.
Action tokens & Pokémon turns
Core
Each trainer gets their action + their active Pokémon action per round. Swapping Pokémon costs the trainer action. Pokémon act on trainer initiative but use their own move set. Pokémon moves map to Daggerheart attack/utility actions — damage, conditions, or narrative effects.
Stress & Pokémon bond
Core
Trainers gain Stress when their Pokémon faints or is in danger. A Pokémon fainting fills 2 Stress. Full Stress = trainer breakdown (narrative consequence, not elimination).
Conditions
Core
Restrained — The target cannot move from their current position and cannot flee combat. The condition ends when another effect removes it. Vulnerable — Attacks against this target have advantage until the condition ends. Stunned — The target cannot take actions or reactions until the condition ends. Hidden — The target cannot be targeted by attacks or abilities until they are revealed or act. Impaired — The target has disadvantage on all action rolls until the condition ends. Threatened — The target has disadvantage on attacks against anyone other than the source of this condition.

Status conditions
Pokémon Rule
Burn — 1d4 damage at start of each turn. Physical damage halved. Paralysis — Roll a die each turn. On an odd result, the Pokémon loses its action. Poison — 1d4 damage per turn, escalating +1 each round (round 1: 1d4, round 2: 1d4+1, round 3: 1d4+2, etc.). Sleep — cannot act. Trainer spends their action and makes an Instinct roll (DC 12) to attempt to wake. Frozen — cannot act. On its turn, the target may roll 2d12 (no attribute modifier) against a Difficulty equal to the attack roll that applied Frozen to shatter the ice and break free; otherwise it thaws automatically after 1d4 rounds. Confused — 50% chance Pokémon hits itself (1d6, no type modifier).
Catching Pokémon
Pokémon Rule

To attempt a catch, the trainer makes an Instinct roll against a DC set by the target Pokémon's Stage: Stage 0 — DC 10, Stage 1 — DC 12, Stage 2 — DC 15, Legendary — varies by encounter, GM discretion. Being in a weakened state (below half HP) or afflicted with a status condition will lower the DC at the GM's discretion — but neither is required to attempt a catch.

A player may push a catch roll (reroll, taking the better result) at the cost of 1 Stress. A critical failure on the catch roll breaks the Ball or lets the Pokémon shake free entirely, ending the attempt — no further pushes are allowed after a critical fail.

Pokémon evolution
Pokémon Rule
Evolution happens at narrative milestones, not just XP thresholds. Triggered by: - Reaching a bond level threshold with their trainer - Completing a meaningful story beat tied to that Pokémon - Using a specific Evolution Item (Stone, held item, trade, etc.) Evolution is a scene. It is never just a stat swap. It should feel earned. Canceling evolution: This is a purely narrative beat, not a roll. If a player wants their Pokémon to hold off evolving, it plays out as the trainer convincing/roleplaying with their Pokémon in the moment — no dice involved.
Pokémon party size
Pokémon Rule
Each player may carry a maximum of 6 Pokémon at a time. This reflects the classic trainer limit and keeps party management meaningful. Pokémon beyond the active party may be stored and swapped out at a Pokémon Center or designated rest point between sessions, at the GM's discretion.
Player health, luck & composure
Core
Trainers are humans — not warriors. Their stats reflect that. Luck replaces Armor. Trainers don't wear protective gear; instead their Luck represents fortune, instinct, and narrowly avoiding harm. Mechanically it functions the same as armor, negating some incoming damage, but narratively it is about being in the right place at the right time. Composure Thresholds replace armor thresholds. Rather than physical toughness, these represent a trainer's mental and emotional resilience under pressure. Thresholds are set by subclass at character creation. Crossing a threshold has narrative consequences — it reflects a trainer becoming shaken, rattled, or distressed. This may impose a penalty to Command rolls or cause their Pokémon to react emotionally (becoming protective, reckless, or distressed depending on their bond level). Training thresholds: During a long rest, a trainer may attempt a Strength or Instinct roll to push their endurance. On a success, their threshold improves for the next session. Repeated successes may result in a permanent increase, at GM discretion.
